## PROJECT: Proact

Company: Deloitte Digital

Industry: Enterprise consulting / internal collaboration tools

Project Type: Internal transformation platform

Link: https://www.eashagc.com/project-new/proact

Related Case Study: Gamification Strategy (developed as part of the Proact platform to improve engagement and retention)

Project Overview: Proact was an internal collaboration and knowledge-sharing platform designed for Deloitte studios during the shift to remote work. Teams were struggling with scattered tools, fragmented information, and reduced visibility across projects. The goal of Proact was to centralise knowledge, improve collaboration, and make internal resources easier to discover and use.

Project Context: During the transition to remote work, collaboration that previously happened organically in studios became fragmented across Slack, Teams, email, and multiple repositories. Knowledge sharing became difficult and productivity dropped as teams struggled to locate resources and maintain visibility across projects.

Problem: Consulting teams relied on multiple disconnected tools to manage project knowledge, communication, and resources. This created:
- lost institutional knowledge
- duplicated work across teams
- poor visibility into available resources
- decreased productivity and collaboration efficiency

Primary Role: Lead Product / UX Designer

Responsibilities:
- Defined the product strategy and experience vision for the platform.
- Led UX design from early discovery through final interface design.
- Mentored junior designers working on the project.
- Facilitated collaboration between product managers, developers, and studio stakeholders.
- Conducted research through surveys, focus groups, and interviews to understand practitioner needs.
- Synthesised research insights into product priorities and MVP scope.
- Designed the core platform experience including dashboard, resource library, project views, and reporting tools.

Key Product Components Designed:
- Centralised resource library
- Project visibility dashboard
- Internal knowledge-sharing system
- Reporting and discovery features
- Structured project views for cross-team collaboration

Key Strategic Decisions:
- Focused the MVP on three critical themes: discovery, visibility, and trust.
- Simplified access to shared resources so teams could find tools quickly.
- Prioritised visibility across teams to reduce duplicated work and improve coordination.
- Designed workflows that mirrored how consulting teams actually collaborated.

Research Conducted:
- Practitioner surveys
- Focus groups
- Stakeholder interviews
- Workflow mapping of studio collaboration patterns

Constraints:
- Rapid transition to remote work environments
- Multiple stakeholder groups with competing priorities
- Existing reliance on fragmented legacy tools
- Need to drive adoption across different studios and teams

Impact: Testing showed measurable improvements in practitioner productivity and efficiency.

Key Metrics:
- 20% increase in productivity among practitioners
- Approximately 9 hours saved per week per practitioner through improved resource discovery and workflow efficiency

Adoption & Growth: Proact was rolled out in phases across studios to allow iterative improvement and adoption refinement. The platform later became trusted across multiple studios and was eventually white-labelled for use by other organisations.

Biggest Challenge: While the platform solved the technical and workflow problems, engagement and sustained usage initially dropped after launch. This revealed that solving operational problems alone was not enough to drive long-term engagement.

Follow-up Innovation: To address declining engagement, I proposed introducing gamification mechanics into the platform to encourage knowledge sharing and participation.
This idea initially faced skepticism but ultimately transformed engagement and retention within the platform.

Related Project: The full design and strategy behind this engagement system is detailed in the **Gamification Strategy case study**, which explores how behavioral design and incentives significantly improved platform retention.

Key Learnings: This project strengthened my ability to:
- structure complex and ambiguous organisational problems
- translate research insights into product strategy
- design systems that support collaboration across large teams
- balance operational efficiency with long-term engagement design



## PROJECT: Proact Gamification Strategy

Parent Project: Proact – Internal collaboration and asset-sharing platform at Deloitte Digital.

Link: https://www.eashagc.com/project-new/proact-2

Project Context: After the Proact MVP launched successfully, the platform initially showed strong results, including a 20% increase in practitioner productivity and nearly nine hours saved per week through improved resource discovery and collaboration.
However, over time engagement began to decline. Because Proact relied on users actively contributing and sharing resources, reduced participation threatened the long-term success of the platform.

Problem: Although the platform solved workflow and discovery issues, it lacked strong behavioural incentives for users to continuously contribute knowledge and resources.
Without sustained participation, the system would lose relevance and adoption could decline.

Primary Role: Lead UX Designer responsible for proposing and designing an engagement strategy to improve platform participation and retention.

Strategic Insight: Through research and observation, I realised that the problem was not usability but motivation. The platform needed mechanisms that recognised contributions and encouraged continued participation.

Proposal: I proposed introducing a gamification layer designed around behavioural design principles. The goal was not to turn the product into a game, but to create subtle incentives that rewarded collaboration and knowledge sharing.

Stakeholder Pushback: Stakeholders initially resisted the idea because they felt gamification might appear unprofessional or gimmicky in an enterprise consulting environment.
Instead of pushing the idea through opinion, I gathered supporting evidence:
- examples of successful gamification in knowledge-sharing platforms
- user research insights showing that many practitioners already engaged with reward-based systems in games like PUBG or Fortnite
- behavioural research demonstrating how recognition systems influence participation
This shifted the conversation from “games in a work tool” to “designing motivation and recognition.”

Gamification Mechanics Introduced:
- Recognition for contributing useful resources
- Reward structures that highlighted collaboration
- Visible indicators of contribution and participation
- Incentives encouraging teams to share knowledge rather than duplicate work

Pilot Test: Stakeholders agreed to run a pilot to validate the idea before committing to a full rollout.

Impact: The pilot proved extremely successful.

Key results included:
- Approximately 60% increase in engagement after introducing the gamification layer
- Significant improvement in contribution rates across teams
- Increased knowledge sharing between consulting studios
- The platform becoming one of the most-used internal tools across the organisation

Long-Term Outcome: Following the success of the pilot, the gamification features were rolled out more broadly across the platform.
Proact eventually became a trusted internal system across multiple studios and was later white-labelled for other consulting organisations.

Key Learnings: This project reinforced several important lessons for me:
- solving usability problems alone does not guarantee long-term engagement
- behavioural design can significantly influence user motivation
- stakeholder skepticism can be overcome through research and evidence
- the best product decisions balance creativity with measurable business impact




## PROJECT: Humana Design System

Company: Humana

Industry: Healthcare / Enterprise Digital Products

Project Type: Large-scale design system migration and UX modernisation

Link: https://www.eashagc.com/project-new/humana

Project Overview: Humana’s design ecosystem had become fragmented over time. More than 300 product screens were tied to outdated workflows, duplicated components, and inconsistent design patterns. Collaboration between designers and engineers had slowed because design tokens and components were out of sync with development.
What initially appeared to be a visual redesign was actually a strategic reset: rebuilding the design system foundation so teams could ship faster and more consistently.

Problem: The existing design system had grown bloated and unreliable:
- Over 300 screens were tied to outdated workflows.
- Figma files were messy and difficult to navigate.
- Components were duplicated and inconsistent.
- Design tokens were not aligned with engineering.
- Designers spent significant time resolving file conflicts instead of designing.

This fragmentation slowed development and eroded trust in the design system.

Primary Role: UX Designer responsible for leading the design transition and system restructuring.

Responsibilities:
- Led the migration from the legacy design environment to a new structured Figma system.
- Conducted a full audit of the existing design ecosystem.
- Defined the visual and interaction direction for the new system.
- Created a phased migration roadmap aligned with developer sprints.
- Rebuilt core design tokens and reusable components.
- Mentored two junior designers working on the system.
- Worked closely with engineers to ensure design decisions aligned with technical implementation.
- Collaborated with product managers to ensure the migration supported business priorities.

Strategic Approach: The migration needed to happen without pausing product delivery. To achieve this:

- Conducted a full system audit to identify what could be migrated, rebuilt, or retired.
- Designed a phased migration plan aligned with engineering sprints.
- Established a new Figma environment with structured libraries and naming conventions.
- Rebuilt foundational components and tokens for scalability.
- Ensured each migration phase delivered usable improvements without disrupting active development.

Execution: The system was rebuilt incrementally rather than all at once.
Key activities included:
- Reworking navigation patterns and interaction behaviours.
- Standardising visual styles across products.
- Rebuilding reusable components.
- Updating and restructuring design libraries.
More than 300 screens were redesigned or rebuilt during the process.

Impact: The new system significantly improved collaboration and product delivery.

Key outcomes:
- Reduced Figma conflicts and file management issues.
- Improved consistency between design and engineering implementation.
- Enabled product teams to build new features more efficiently.
- Established a scalable design system foundation for future products.
The migration turned what could have been a disruptive redesign into a steady transformation of Humana’s design practice.

Biggest Challenge: The largest challenge was balancing transformation with ongoing product delivery. The team could not pause releases while the system was rebuilt, so the migration had to happen in parallel with development without disrupting active projects.

Mentorship & Team Impact: During the project I mentored two junior designers, pairing with them on system components and reviewing their work. Over time they became confident contributors to the system, strengthening the design team and distributing ownership of the new design framework.

Key Learnings: This project reinforced several lessons:

- redesigning at scale requires strong coordination between design, engineering, and product teams
- system migrations must be phased carefully to avoid disrupting delivery
- design systems succeed only when teams trust and adopt them
- mentorship and team alignment are as important as technical design decisions


## PROJECT: IVAS – Research Collaboration Platform

Industry: Academic research / knowledge collaboration

Project Type: Concept platform for improving visibility and collaboration among non-STEM researchers

Link: https://www.eashagc.com/project-new/ivas

Project Overview: IVAS was designed to address a common challenge in non-STEM academia: research projects often exist in silos, making collaboration difficult and causing researchers to unknowingly duplicate work.
I led the end-to-end design of IVAS, a platform concept focused on making academic work more visible, trusted, and easier to discover across institutions.
The project explored how a structured digital system could improve research discovery, collaboration opportunities, and knowledge sharing across academic communities.

Project Duration: Approximately four months

Problem: Non-STEM researchers often struggle to discover existing projects or collaborators outside their immediate networks.
Common issues included:
- research projects remaining invisible outside departments
- scattered repositories and resource storage
- difficulty discovering ongoing work at other institutions
- duplicated research efforts due to lack of visibility

Primary Role: Lead Product / UX Designer

Responsibilities:
- Led the full product design process from research through concept design.
- Conducted surveys, interviews, and focus groups with academics.
- Mapped research workflows and collaboration pain points.
- Designed the product architecture and platform interaction flows.
- Defined the MVP scope and long-term platform vision.

Research Conducted:
- Academic surveys across multiple institutions
- Researcher interviews
- Focus groups exploring collaboration patterns
- Workflow mapping of research discovery and resource sharing

Engagement & Early Validation: The concept resonated strongly with researchers.
Early discussions involved:
- more than 20 academic institutions
- over 200 researchers and academics participating in conversations and validation

Strategic Product Vision: IVAS was designed as a platform that could bring together research visibility, collaboration, and trust.
Core platform capabilities included:
- structured researcher profiles
- discoverable project repositories
- collaboration opportunities between institutions
- shared knowledge resources

AI-Aware Opportunities: A key opportunity explored within the platform was using intelligent systems to improve research discovery.
Potential AI-driven features included:
- semantic tagging of research topics to improve search accuracy
- recommendation systems that suggest relevant collaborators
- automated categorisation of research projects
- intelligent discovery of related work across institutions
These capabilities would allow the platform to surface relevant research connections that traditional search tools often miss.

Key Design Decisions:
- Focused the MVP on visibility and discovery before adding advanced collaboration tools.
- Designed structured research profiles to help academics present work clearly.
- Created project repositories that allow work to be browsed across institutions.
- Prioritised trust and credibility mechanisms to ensure research quality.

Biggest Challenge: Academic collaboration is shaped by institutional structures and cultural habits. Designing a platform that could fit different research workflows required balancing flexibility with structure.

Impact: While IVAS was a concept project, it generated significant early interest and validation within academic communities.
The project demonstrated strong demand for tools that improve visibility and collaboration among non-STEM researchers.

Key Learnings:This project reinforced several lessons:

- visibility is one of the biggest barriers to collaboration
- platforms succeed when they reduce discovery friction
- research ecosystems behave like knowledge networks rather than simple databases
- intelligent discovery systems (including AI) can dramatically improve collaboration opportunities
